\section{Gaps and Contribution}

We can state the central finding plainly: nobody has built it yet. No existing Indian legal AI system connects precedent retrieval to case fact structuring, structuring to argument graph construction, argument graphs to outcome prediction, or prediction to counterfactual what-if analysis. Each axis we surveyed operates in its own silo. Retrieved precedents are never routed to a structuring module. Structured facts and extracted entities stop at the output layer---no argument graph constructor picks them up. Prediction models run without argument-level context, and their outputs are never fed into any counterfactual reasoning component. The closest the field has come to integration is coupling retrieval with generation~\cite{nigam-etal-2025-nyayarag} or with case-based reasoning~\cite{wiratunga-etal-2024-cbrrag}, but even those systems touch only two of the five axes. We think this fragmentation is no longer a reflection of the field's youth---it is a design choice the community has made by default, and one that needs to be made deliberately instead.

At the task level, three gaps stand out. Argument graph construction for Indian legal judgments has no published dataset, no trained model, and no evaluation benchmark. It is, by any measure, the largest structural blind spot in the field. Counterfactual what-if analysis has not been attempted at all in the Indian context, even though it is arguably the capability most relevant to practising lawyers---the people these systems are ostensibly being built for. And evaluation remains systematically tilted toward Supreme Court data~\cite{malik-etal-2021-ildc, nigam-etal-2024-predex}. District and high courts, which together account for the overwhelming bulk of India's pending caseload, lack reliable benchmarks. LegalEval's annotation scope reflects this same imbalance~\cite{modi-etal-2023-semeval}.

The data-level picture is no better. No annotated resource captures argument-level structure---the claim-evidence-statute-precedent linkages that argument graph construction would require. Multilingual annotations are absent entirely; Hindi and regional language passages embedded in English judgments remain invisible to every model we surveyed. The large-scale corpora that enable LLM fine-tuning, NyayaAnumana chief among them~\cite{nigam-etal-2025-nyayaanumana}, trade annotation depth for volume. PredEx offers expert-level explanations but at a fraction of the scale~\cite{nigam-etal-2024-predex}. No single resource delivers both. We suspect that until one does, the field will continue producing systems that are either large-scale but shallow, or deep but narrow.

We should be clear about what this paper contributes and what it does not. It is a survey. We provide the first structured review of Indian legal NLP organised around five functional capability axes, map the existing literature to each axis, catalogue the datasets and evaluation methods in use, and identify what remains unsolved. Our primary claim is that individual capabilities have matured enough to make integration feasible---and that the absence of integration is now the binding constraint on meaningful progress. We hope this survey serves as a useful reference for anyone aiming to build toward a unified Indian legal AI architecture, but we make no claim that building one will be easy.

\section{Ethical Considerations}

Train a model on historical Indian court judgments and it will learn what those judgments contain---including their biases. Outcome disparities correlated with gender, caste, economic status, and geography are well documented in Indian legal history. A prediction system optimised to reproduce historical outcome distributions will reproduce historical inequities along exactly these lines, unless someone intervenes during training and evaluation. The problem is not unique to India; bias amplification has been flagged across jurisdictions~\cite{medvedeva-etal-2020-echr}. But the stakes escalate sharply when model outputs start influencing real legal decisions, because there is a genuine risk that statistical artefacts get mistaken for legal reasoning by users who trust the system more than they should. We believe bias auditing across demographic and geographic subgroups should be a non-negotiable prerequisite for deployment---not a post-launch afterthought.

Explainability, in a legal setting, is not a feature. It is a procedural obligation. Parties to litigation have a right to understand the basis of decisions that affect their legal interests, and that right does not evaporate when part of the decision process is automated~\cite{deakin-etal-2023-xai-law}. This creates a real problem for the field, because the models that predict best are the ones that explain least. A generated paragraph that reads fluently and cites the right statutes may still be legally incoherent---and no automated metric will catch that. PredEx's approach, grounding explanation evaluation in expert annotation rather than ROUGE scores~\cite{nigam-etal-2024-predex}, is the right direction. But the community needs to internalise a harder lesson: fluency is not soundness. A model that writes well but reasons poorly is more dangerous, not less, because its confidence is convincing.

One distinction cannot be blurred. Everything we have surveyed in this paper---retrieval systems, structuring modules, prediction models, explanation generators---belongs in the category of decision-support tools. They organise information, surface precedents, structure arguments, and estimate outcomes so that human legal professionals can reason more effectively. They are not autonomous adjudicators, and positioning them as such would be irresponsible. The most capable systems currently available still exhibit significant limitations in coverage, interpretability, and generalisation that rule out unsupervised deployment~\cite{nigam-etal-2025-nyayaanumana, mersha-etal-2024-xai}. Building Indian legal AI responsibly will require sustained collaboration among NLP researchers, legal professionals, and policymakers. The goal is augmentation, not automation---and losing sight of that distinction is the fastest way to erode the procedural safeguards that the rule of law depends on.
