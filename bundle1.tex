\begin{abstract}
Close to fifty million cases sit unresolved across India's three-tier court hierarchy. That number alone makes a strong case for computational intervention, yet the legal AI tools available today are built in silos---retrieval engines that know nothing about prediction models, prediction models that ignore explainability, and explainability modules that never feed back into retrieval. We survey Indian legal NLP through five capability axes: precedent search, case fact structuring, argument graph construction, judicial outcome simulation, and what-if analysis. For each axis we catalogue representative methods, benchmark datasets, and evaluation practices. Our principal observation is blunt: no existing system ties all five axes together. Argument graph construction and counterfactual what-if reasoning are, for practical purposes, absent from the Indian legal AI landscape. We argue that progress on individual axes has reached a point of diminishing returns without a unifying pipeline that couples retrieval, structured reasoning, prediction, and interactive analysis.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

\IEEEPARstart{I}{ndia's} courts are drowning. Roughly fifty million cases remain pending across district courts, high courts, and the Supreme Court, and the backlog grows faster than the judiciary can clear it. This is not a scheduling problem; it is a structural failure of access to justice that periodic administrative reform has proven unable to fix. Litigants wait years---sometimes a full decade---for resolution. Public trust erodes quietly, case by case. AI-assisted legal tools offer a way to augment, not replace, judicial reasoning at a scale that human capacity alone cannot match. Over one million Supreme Court and High Court judgments are now digitised~\cite{nigam-etal-2025-nyayaanumana}, providing an empirical base that few other common-law jurisdictions can rival. We believe these conditions make India one of the most consequential---and still underexplored---testbeds for legal AI research.

The tools that do exist, however, talk past each other. A retrieval engine that surfaces relevant precedents knows nothing about the prediction model sitting one API call away. The prediction model, for its part, generates a disposition label without any awareness of how the retrieval module ranked its candidates. Explainability components, where they exist at all, bolt on after the fact. This fragmentation is not how judges reason. A judge does not first retrieve, then apply statute, then weigh facts; she does all three simultaneously, circling back when a new precedent reshapes the factual picture. Recent empirical evidence supports this intuition: coupling retrieval with downstream reasoning outperforms strict pipeline decomposition~\cite{nigam-etal-2025-nyayarag}, and case-based reasoning integrated into retrieval-augmented generation produces answers that are measurably more grounded in law~\cite{wiratunga-etal-2024-cbrrag}. The lesson is clear---or should be---that tight integration across capability boundaries is not optional.

We should be upfront about what this paper is. It is a survey, not a system. We make two contributions: a structured review of five capability areas in Indian legal NLP, and the explicit identification of the missing integrated multi-capability pipeline as the field's central open problem. The five axes are: (1)~precedent search---sparse and dense retrieval of prior judgments; (2)~case fact structuring---named entity recognition, rhetorical role labelling, and statutory section tagging; (3)~argument graph construction---extraction of claims, evidence, and their relational structure; (4)~judicial outcome simulation---predicting dispositions with natural-language explanations; and (5)~what-if analysis---counterfactual hypothesis testing over case facts. These axes, taken together, span the full reasoning chain from information retrieval to interactive deliberation.

The rest of this paper follows a straightforward path. Section~II sets up background on the Indian legal ecosystem and the specific headaches it creates for NLP. Sections~III through VII walk through each capability axis in turn. Section~VIII pulls back to compare across axes, flagging integration gaps and recurring themes. Section~IX takes up evaluation methodologies. Section~X outlines open problems and future directions. Section~XI wraps up.

\section{Background: The Indian Legal Ecosystem}

India inherited its common law tradition from British colonial rule, and that inheritance carries real weight: judicial decisions are precedent, not merely advisory. Judicial reasoning here rests on three pillars that a court must hold in mind at once---the established facts, the applicable statutes (principally the Indian Penal Code and the Code of Civil Procedure), and binding prior rulings invoked through stare decisis. No competent judge consults these sequentially. A judgment forms at the intersection of all three: facts are read through the lens of statute, statutes are interpreted in light of prior rulings, and prior rulings are tested against the factual matrix of the case at hand. We contend that any computational framework that treats these pillars as independent inputs to a staged pipeline has already compromised its fidelity to how law actually works.

Processing Indian legal text introduces a set of headaches that researchers working on ECHR or U.S. federal case law rarely encounter. The most obvious is multilingualism. A single judgment may drift between English, Hindi, Urdu, and a regional language mid-paragraph, with no markup or translation cues---a nightmare for tokenisers trained on monolingual corpora. Formatting is another pain point. There is no standard template across Indian courts; judgments from different benches, different decades, and different levels of the hierarchy vary wildly in structure, citation conventions, and rhetorical organisation. Rule-based extraction breaks almost immediately. And then there is annotation scarcity. The LegalEval shared task laid this bare: even trained annotators disagreed substantially on rhetorical role labels for Indian legal text~\cite{modi-etal-2023-semeval}. General-purpose encoders like base BERT are not up to the task either. InLegalBERT showed that domain-adapted pre-training on Indian corpora is essentially non-negotiable for competitive performance~\cite{paul-etal-2023-inlegalbert}. We suspect the community still underestimates how much domain shift matters in this setting.

The good news is that resource creation has accelerated sharply. ILDC gave the community its first large-scale benchmark---over thirty-five thousand Supreme Court cases annotated for judgment prediction and explanation~\cite{malik-etal-2021-ildc}. PredEx raised the bar by providing expert-annotated explanations tied to specific textual spans within judgments~\cite{nigam-etal-2024-predex}. NyayaAnumana, the most recent and largest corpus, spans multiple court levels and ships with its own domain-specialised language model~\cite{nigam-etal-2025-nyayaanumana}. These datasets have driven measurable gains on tasks like binary judgment prediction and rhetorical role classification. But here is the catch. Every one of them targets a single capability axis in isolation. No existing work unifies retrieval, structuring, reasoning, prediction, and interactive analysis under one coherent architecture. That gap---the absence of integration---is what motivates the five-axis taxonomy we develop in the sections that follow.
