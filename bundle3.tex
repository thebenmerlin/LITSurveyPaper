\section{Judicial Outcome Simulation}

Prediction has the longest research history in the field, moving from basic classifiers to the current LLM state-of-the-art. The task is straightforward: take the text of a case and predict if it was accepted or dismissed. If you can, generate the reasoning too. We are currently at a transition point where the community is realizing that accuracy numbers, taken alone, are nearly meaningless in a legal context. If a model predicts the right outcome for the wrong legal reasons, it isn't a tool---it's a liability.

\subsection{Classical and Early Neural Approaches}

The early days were defined by manual feature engineering. Researchers spent months hand-crafting indicators for bag-of-words or TF-IDF models. They worked, sort of. But they were brittle, failing the moment they saw a case from a different court or a different decade. Neural networks, specifically CNNs and RNNs, removed the need for manual features but hit a different wall: document length. Indian judgments are long. Really long. Most early recurrent models simply ran out of memory or lost the context by the time they reached the operative order. LegalBERT was the first real breakthrough here, proving that domain shift is so severe in law that general-purpose embeddings are essentially a different language.

\subsection{Transformer-Based Prediction}

Transformers changed what was possible. By leveraging self-attention, models like InLegalBERT could finally capture the dependencies between a factual assertion on page two and a statutory application on page twenty~\cite{paul-etal-2023-inlegalbert}. The ILDC benchmark made this evaluation systematic for the first time, providing 35,000 cases to test these architectures~\cite{malik-etal-2021-ildc}. However, the 512-token limit of base BERT remains a constant frustration. We have seen researchers try everything from overlapping chunks to sliding windows, but we suspect this ceiling is more damaging than current evaluations reveal. You cannot model a 100-page high court judgment by looking at it through a series of disconnected 500-word keyholes.

\subsection{Explainability as a First-Class Requirement}

A model that outputs ``dismissed'' without a reason is useless to a lawyer. You cannot audit it, you cannot identify its biases, and you certainly cannot build trust with it. PredEx addresses this by providing 15,000 cases where the rationales aren't just model-generated hallucinations, but are actual explanations written by legal experts~\cite{nigam-etal-2024-predex}. This moves the goalpost from ``does the model look right?'' to ``is the model grounded in expert reasoning?'' We observe, however, that even these expert annotations are often compressed summaries. Does a summary truly capture the recursive, self-correcting nature of a judge's internal reasoning?

\subsection{Large Language Models and the Scale Frontier}

Scale has become the default answer to everything in NLP, and legal AI is no exception. NyayaAnumana and its specialized model, INLegalLlama, represent the current ceiling for Indian legal prediction~\cite{nigam-etal-2025-nyayaanumana}. These models internalize the rhythms of judicial language across 700,000 case proceedings. But scale has brought a new problem: benchmark bias. Many of our current test sets are cherry-picked from cases with unambiguous outcomes, which makes the models look much better than they actually are in the messy reality of a contested courtroom~\cite{nigam-etal-2024-rethinking}. We believe benchmark bias is the single most underappreciated methodological issue in the field today.

\subsection{Remaining Limitations}

Three gaps remain. First, prediction models are still built in isolation from the retrieval and structuring axes we discussed earlier. There is no pathway for a structured fact to inform a final prediction. 

Second, the data is too Supreme Court heavy. District courts are where the real backlog lives, yet they have almost no presence in our benchmarks.

Third, the evaluation metrics are often lazy. Accuracy is the easiest thing to measure, but in law, it is also the most misleading. If your model achieves 90\% accuracy but fails every time a witness is deemed unreliable, it isn't just imperfect---it is dangerous because nobody has checked those specific failure modes.

\section{What-If Analysis}

What-if analysis is the capability that practitioners actually want, yet there is almost no literature behind it. It is also, in our view, the one that matters most. While prediction tells you what the outcome is, and explainability tells you why, what-if analysis asks what would happen if the facts were different. For a lawyer preparing a case, counterfactual reasoning is the primary form of explainability that matters.

\subsection{Counterfactual NLP}

In standard NLP, this usually means perturbing an input to see when the label flips. If I change a ``dowry demand'' to a ``loan request,'' does the conviction stand? This is not just a technical curiosity. It is a way to map the decision boundaries of a model and identify which facts are actually driving the outcome. We distinguish between forward-looking what-if analysis (testing hypotheses) and backward-looking counterfactuals (explaining past decisions).

\subsection{XAI in Legal Decision-Making}

The legal system has a transparency requirement that most AI systems cannot meet. You have a right to know why your interest was affected, especially if an algorithm helped decide it~\cite{deakin-etal-2023-xai-law}. Most current XAI methods like LIME or SHAP are designed for feature importance, not the structured counterfactuals that legal reasoning requires. Until systems can answer ``what if,'' their explanations will remain descriptive rather than prescriptive.

\subsection{The Indian Gap}

There is no what-if analysis system for Indian law. We have found zero published papers on the topic. This is a massive opportunity for the community, especially given the resource constraints in Indian litigation.

A tool that lets a lawyer test different statutory framings or fact patterns without needing a new dataset every time would be transformative. It is the logical endpoint of the integration we are proposing.

Our survey ends here because the literature ends here. We cannot review what hasn't been built, but we can point to the empty space and call it a priority.
