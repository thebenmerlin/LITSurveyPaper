\section{Datasets}

Datasets have been the bottleneck. Western legal AI had the luxury of inheriting decades of structured case law from repositories like LexisNexis and PACER; Indian legal NLP has had to build its benchmarks from scratch, one annotation campaign at a time, typically relying on legal professionals whose time is expensive and scarce. We survey the seven principal datasets available as of 2025. They differ sharply in scale, court coverage, annotation depth, and public availability, and understanding those differences is essential for evaluating where the field actually stands versus where it claims to be.

The jump from ILDC's 35,000 cases to NyayaAnumana's 700,000 is not incremental---it is a qualitative shift that makes LLM-scale fine-tuning feasible for Indian legal text for the first time~\cite{nigam-etal-2025-nyayaanumana}. But scale and depth are not the same thing. NyayaAnumana gives you outcome labels and court-level metadata; it does not give you the expert-written natural language explanations that PredEx provides at a fraction of the size~\cite{nigam-etal-2024-predex}. We would argue that any claim about ``state-of-the-art'' Indian legal judgment prediction needs to specify whether the model was trained with explanation supervision or without it, because the two settings produce fundamentally different systems. District court data, meanwhile, is almost invisible across all annotated datasets---a glaring omission given that district courts handle the vast majority of India's pending caseload. The InLegalBERT corpus and Indian Kanoon offer raw text at scale, but with no task-specific annotations their use is limited to pre-training and unsupervised exploration.

Three gaps stand out, and none of the seven datasets we surveyed addresses any of them. There are no argument-level annotations linking claims to evidence, statutes, and precedents within a single judgment. Without them, argument graph construction (Section~V) is forced to operate in an unsupervised or weakly supervised mode. There are no multilingual annotations that handle the Hindi--English or regional-language code-mixing that pervades Indian judicial text, despite the fact that this code-mixing is not rare---it is routine. And there are no counterfactual annotations of the kind a what-if analysis system (Section~VII) would need for training or evaluation. Each of these gaps maps directly onto an open research direction we have flagged earlier. Closing them will require annotation campaigns that bring NLP researchers and legal domain experts together in ways the community has not yet managed at scale~\cite{modi-etal-2023-semeval}.

\section{Evaluation Metrics}

How you measure a system matters at least as much as how you build it, and this is a point that legal AI papers tend to gloss over. Pick the wrong metric and you end up optimising for something the legal system does not care about---or worse, producing a model that looks excellent on a leaderboard but fails catastrophically on the cases that matter most. The majority of legal AI studies report accuracy as their sole measure, without justifying that choice or acknowledging what it hides~\cite{medvedeva-etal-2020-echr}. On imbalanced datasets---and Indian legal datasets are almost always imbalanced---accuracy is close to meaningless. We survey the metrics used across the retrieval, classification, and generation tasks covered in this paper, and where appropriate, we flag where metric choice has led the field astray.

\subsection{Retrieval Metrics}

MAP (Mean Average Precision) remains the default for ranked retrieval evaluation. It computes precision at each relevant document in the ranked list, averages across the list, and then averages again across queries---yielding a single number that captures both how many relevant documents were retrieved and how high they were ranked. nDCG (Normalised Discounted Cumulative Gain) complements MAP by applying a logarithmic discount to lower-ranked results, which aligns well with how lawyers actually search: they look at the first page, maybe the second, and rarely go further. Both BM25 evaluations~\cite{rosa-etal-2021-bm25} and dense retrieval experiments in COLIEE~\cite{althammer-etal-2021-dossier} report MAP and nDCG as primary measures.

The catch---and we think this is underappreciated---is that both metrics require relevance judgments. In legal retrieval, relevance is not a surface-level property. Whether a 1987 Supreme Court ruling on tenant eviction is ``relevant'' to a 2022 high court dispute over landlord obligations is a question of legal interpretation, not keyword overlap. Collecting those judgments at scale requires legal expertise, which makes evaluation itself bottlenecked by the same annotation scarcity that constrains training data.

\subsection{Classification, Generation, and Explainability Metrics}

Accuracy and macro-averaged F1 are the standard pair for binary and multi-class outcome prediction. On balanced data, accuracy is fine. Indian legal datasets are rarely balanced. Dismissals dominate most corpora, which means a classifier that predicts ``dismissed'' every time can post a high accuracy score without having learned anything at all~\cite{medvedeva-etal-2020-echr}. Macro-F1 corrects for this by weighting each class equally, and we would argue it should be the primary metric in every legal judgment prediction paper---not a secondary one buried in an appendix.

NER evaluation requires a finer granularity. Aggregate F1 across all entity types can hide the fact that a model performs well on common categories (dates, court names) while completely failing on rare but legally critical ones like witness names and provision numbers~\cite{kalamkar-etal-2022-ner}. Per-entity-type F1 is the minimum standard; anything less is misleading.

Explanation evaluation is harder, because the thing being measured---legal soundness---is not something an automated metric can assess. ROUGE and BERTScore capture surface overlap with reference text, which tells you whether the generated explanation uses similar words. It tells you nothing about whether the reasoning is correct. Expert Likert-scale assessments, where legal professionals rate adequacy, accuracy, and completeness on structured scales, remain the only reliable approach. Both INLegalLlama~\cite{nigam-etal-2025-nyayaanumana} and PredEx~\cite{nigam-etal-2024-predex} adopted this methodology. It is expensive. It does not scale well. And it is, as far as we can tell, irreplaceable---which is a problem the community has not yet confronted honestly.
